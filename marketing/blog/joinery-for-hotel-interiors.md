# Joinery for hotel interiors: what changes when manufacturing is in the same team

Most hotel interiors have a joinery problem that nobody labels as one. The design team specifies millwork in detail. The main contractor appoints a joinery sub-contractor. That sub-contractor sources materials, manufactures panels, and delivers to site where a separate installation team takes over. If the site dimensions have shifted between survey and delivery, the adjustment happens on the floor, in real time, by whoever is nearest. If the veneer lot does not match the approved sample, the conversation travels backwards through three or four handoffs before anyone with the authority to resolve it is involved.

This is the standard model. It works well enough on projects where the joinery is simple and the tolerance for variation is wide. On hotel interiors, where the millwork runs from lobby to suite to back-of-house and the guest experience depends on the quality of the built-in environment, it creates a specific class of problem: the gap between what was specified and what gets installed is wide, and accountability for that gap is distributed across parties who each have a partial view of what went wrong.

---

## What joinery covers in a hotel project

Built-in joinery in a hotel interior is more extensive than the term suggests. It includes the cabinetry and storage systems in every guest room, the custom millwork in lobbies and circulation spaces, the reception desk and any front-of-house service counters, the panelling and feature walls that carry the guest's first impression of the space, and the back-of-house joinery that supports the operational demands of a property running three hundred and sixty-five days a year.

Each of these elements has different durability requirements, different materiality, and different installation tolerances. Guest room wardrobes need to close quietly and stay aligned after years of daily use. Lobby millwork needs to hold its surface quality through cycles of cleaning and contact that furniture designers rarely specify for. Back-of-house counters and storage need to absorb the kind of use that opening photographs never show. Specifying all of this as a coherent programme, rather than as a list of separate line items, is how the interior holds together over time.

---

## Where the multi-contractor model creates rework

When joinery manufacture and installation are separated, and when neither sits with the design team, the programme has three structural weak points.

The first is survey. Joinery manufactured off-site to design drawings is calibrated to the building as it was designed, not as it was built. Hotels are constructed to tolerances that are acceptable for structure and services but tight for millwork. When the survey happens late, or when the manufacturer does not survey directly and works only from drawings, the site adjustment is made during installation by cutting and packing. The result is visible.

The second is materials. A joinery contractor who sources veneers and laminates from third parties is buying from available stock. If the specified material is unavailable, a substitute is found. If the substitute is approved by procurement rather than by the design team, it may be dimensionally identical and chromatically close enough to pass review, but it will not match the batch already installed in the adjacent room. In a hotel, where the same finish runs across three hundred rooms, batch consistency is not a secondary concern.

The third is fire compliance. Built-in joinery in a hotel is subject to fire performance requirements that are non-negotiable and that apply to the complete assembly, not to the substrate alone. A fire-rated panel that is modified on site, or that is assembled with a non-compliant adhesive, or that carries an applied finish not covered by its test certificate, loses its certification. Retrofitting compliance after installation is expensive and time-consuming. Designing it in at the specification stage costs nothing extra.

## What in-house manufacturing changes for the programme

We manufacture joinery through Hi Mobilya, our in-house production facility. The workshop produces to the same brief as the design team because it is the same team. Survey data flows directly from our installation crew to the workshop floor. Material selections are reviewed against production capability before the specification is fixed. The batch of veneer for the suites and the batch for the lobby are sourced together and logged against the same project record.

The operational consequence is that the gap between specification and delivery is narrower, not because we are more diligent than third-party contractors, but because the communication path is shorter. A dimension change on site does not travel through a sub-contractor's project manager and a main contractor's coordinator before it reaches the person who can action it. It travels one step.

This matters on hotel projects because the programme does not flex. The opening date is set. Every delay in the joinery programme affects the rooms that follow it: floor finishes, FF&E placement, snagging, commissioning. A joinery programme that runs to its milestones is not a nice-to-have. It is what keeps the rest of the fit-out on schedule.

## Material and finish integration from the same team

The quality of a built-in joinery element in a hotel interior is determined almost entirely by the quality of the material specification and the consistency of its application. A well-constructed carcass finished with an inconsistent veneer or an unstable lacquer will show its age faster than a simpler piece finished correctly.

SILADU, our materials and surfaces operation, handles veneer sourcing, surface development, and finish specification. It works alongside the joinery manufacturing floor, which means that material decisions are tested against production knowledge before the specification is sent to the workshop. Which veneer species hold their colour under UV exposure in a south-facing suite. Which surface treatments survive the cleaning protocols used by the housekeeping team. Which profiles can be achieved with the machinery on the floor without creating an edge condition that will chip.

The result is a specification that closes at production, not one that requires interpretation during manufacturing and adjustment during installation.

## Fire compliance as a design decision, not a certification exercise

Every piece of built-in joinery in a hotel lobby, corridor, or guest room is subject to fire performance requirements under local building codes. These requirements apply to the complete assembly: the substrate, the adhesives, the finish, and any modifications made during installation.

A joinery programme that is not designed to these requirements from the start will hit compliance at the certification stage, after the installation is complete, and any remediation required at that point is both expensive and disruptive. The most common failure modes are not structural: they are surface finishes applied over test-certified panels that are not covered by the panel's certificate, or site modifications that invalidate the certified assembly configuration.

We specify joinery to the applicable fire requirements during design development, before manufacturing begins. The certification path is confirmed before material selection is fixed. Site installation follows the certified configuration. This saves rework, and it saves the schedule risk that comes with a late-stage compliance finding on a property that has a fixed opening date.

## Back-of-house joinery: the part that determines operational durability

The guest-facing millwork gets the specification attention. The back-of-house joinery is where the property's operational durability is won or lost.

Reception desks, service counters, beverage stations, and storage systems in hotel back-of-house carry a usage load that front-of-house millwork does not. They are accessed by staff rather than guests, which means they are used faster and maintained less carefully. A reception desk drawer that sticks after eighteen months of eight-hour shift use does not appear in any guest survey, but it affects the speed and accuracy of every check-in interaction the property has.

Back-of-house joinery specified and manufactured to the same standard as front-of-house is not a premium. It is a maintenance cost that does not appear in the first three years and then appears all at once.

## What to ask before you appoint your joinery contractor

If you are specifying joinery for a hotel fit-out, the questions that determine programme and quality are not about aesthetics. They are about who manufactures, who surveys, who is responsible for the fire compliance path, and who is accountable when the installed result diverges from the approved specification.

Does the contractor manufacture in-house, or do they manage a sub-contractor? If they manage a sub, who holds the programme risk when the sub-contractor's delivery slips? Who surveys the building, and when? How is material batch consistency managed across the full scope of the project? Who owns the fire compliance documentation, and what happens if an installation modification affects the certified assembly?

A joinery programme that starts with clear answers to these questions is one that ends at handover rather than in a remediation conversation six months after opening.
