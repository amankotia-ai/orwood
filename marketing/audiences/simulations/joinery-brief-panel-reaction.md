## Per-Persona Reactions

Each persona was exposed to the brief independently.

### 1. VP Development, GCC Hospitality Group
**Segment:** Hospitality principals (weight: 9, highest)

**First impression:** Relief. This reads like a contractor that has actually delivered hotels, not just claimed to. "No handoff, no margin stack, no accountability gap" is exactly the promise this persona has been burned on before.

**Comprehension in their words:** "They make the joinery in-house and use the same team for materials and installation. That means if something's wrong at handover, I'm not chasing three different suppliers."

**Resonance score: 3 / 3**

What lands:
- "One accountable partner for design, supply, and installation" — direct match for their primary JTBD
- "Delivered on schedule as part of one team" — the pre-opening date is everything
- "No cost surprises" — addresses board scrutiny on overruns
- 200+ projects across 5-star hotels — credible proof at the right scale
- Hi Mobilya named as the manufacturer — reduces the fear that "in-house" is a marketing claim

**Objections:**
- "200 projects total is not the same as 200 rooms in a single project. Can they handle our scale?"
- "Where are the named references? '5-star hotels' tells me nothing I can verify."
- "What is the lead time from Hi Mobilya to GCC? Who handles customs and logistics?"
- "What does 'no cost surprises' mean contractually — fixed price, or GMP?"

**What's missing that would convince them:**
- Named hotel group or branded property references (with permission to call)
- GCC local presence or installation team
- Lead time claim for the region
- Rooms-per-project scale evidence

**Likely next action:** Enquiry, if ORWOOD can produce one or two references in their geography. Without that, continues evaluating.

**Synthetic verbatims:**
- "The owned manufacturing story is the right one. I don't want to hear 'we manage your joinery contractor.' What I need is the hotel references to show my CEO."
- "No handoff, no margin stack — that's written by someone who has been in a post-opening defect meeting. I want to talk to them."
- "200+ projects means nothing until I know which hotel groups they've worked with."

**Comprehension in their words:** "They do custom joinery to fit any dimension, including non-standard ones. They seem to manufacture themselves. I want to know if they have experience in older buildings where nothing is square."

**Resonance score: 2 / 3**

- "Modular or fixed installations, scaled from single rooms to multi-floor projects" — signals they can work at smaller scope

### 3. Head of Real Estate, Corporate Developer — Class-A Offices
**Segment:** Commercial property owners and developers (weight: 7)

**First impression:** Solid, but hospitality-oriented. The commercial mention exists in the brief but the emphasis is clearly hotels. "Fire compliance built-in" is the strongest hook for this persona — it is a genuine pain point, and no one in the market frames it this way.

**Comprehension in their words:** "Single-source contractor for joinery, materials, and installation. Fire compliance is handled at design stage, not certification stage. One invoice, one warranty."

**Resonance score: 2 / 3**

What lands:
- "Fire-rated joinery where required (compliance with local building codes)" — addresses certification headache
- "Fire compliance built-in: saves rework and schedule risk" — this is a real problem this persona faces
- "One supplier, one invoice, one warranty" — consolidation argument resonates in a multi-trade environment
- "Reception desks, service counters, back-of-house joinery" — this is their scope

**Objections:**
- "What certifications specifically? Which fire test standards? I need documents, not claims."
- "Can you do Cat B joinery at compressed timelines? 12 to 16 weeks is what we're working to."
- "Where is your nearest team? Defect-liability response time matters."
- "Nothing here about sustainable or WELL-adjacent finishes. Institutional tenants ask for this."

**What's missing that would convince them:**
- Certificate names and test standards (not just "fire-rated")
- Timeline claim for commercial fit-out
- Defect-liability period
- Sustainability or ESG-adjacent credentials

**Likely next action:** Adds to RFP longlist but needs certification documentation before shortlisting.

**Synthetic verbatims:**
- "Fire compliance built-in is genuinely interesting — I have never seen a joinery contractor lead with that. But I need the test certificates, not the claim."
- "The single accountability model maps well to how we structure contracts. I want to see if the scope taxonomy matches our RFP line items."
- "Commercial is listed as a vertical, but the whole brief reads like hotels. Show me a commercial lobby that's been delivered."

**What's missing that would convince them:**
- Specific tolerance claims (numerical)
- Mock-up panel process described
- Case study imagery showing detail-level execution, not room-level

**Likely next action:** Would visit the factory or request a sample panel. Not ready to specify without physical proof.

**Synthetic verbatims:**

## Synthesis

### Where all segments agree

**The owned-manufacturing claim (Hi Mobilya) is the brief's strongest asset.** Every persona — from the developer who fears cost overruns to the architect who has been burned by tolerance failures — responds positively to the specificity. Naming Hi Mobilya converts "we manufacture in-house" from a marketing phrase into a verifiable fact. This is the differentiation that should lead.

**The single-accountability model lands with every commercial audience.** "No handoff, no margin stack, no accountability gap" reads as credible to the hospitality principal, the commercial developer, and the procurement manager alike. It is the clearest articulation of ORWOOD's moat in plain language.

**Fire compliance built-in is a genuine surprise.** The commercial developer and procurement manager react strongly — it is a real pain point that no other contractor in the market frames as a capability at the design stage rather than a certification problem. The brief mentions it, but it is buried in a table. It deserves earlier placement.

### Where segments split

**The brief excels at commercial logic; it fails at craft language.** Hospitality principals (resonance 3) and procurement managers (resonance 3) fully engage. Architects and commercial developers (resonance 2) are interested but need proof rather than claim. Interior designers (resonance 1) and UHNW homeowners (resonance 1) are largely not addressed — they need to see materials and craft celebrated, not listed.

**SILADU is the brief's most underexplained asset.** It is mentioned multiple times across the brief but never explained. The architect finds it intriguing. The interior designer is frustrated. The procurement manager treats it as a named entity, which is useful. The homeowner ignores it. A single sentence explaining what SILADU is and what it gives the specifier would unlock the brief for the two highest-craft audiences.

**Scale reads as both proof and barrier.** The 200-project, 15-country claim gives confidence to the GCC VP and the procurement manager. It actively concerns the boutique hotel owner and the UHNW homeowner, who worry they will be deprioritised. The brief has no acknowledgement of this tension.

|---|---|
| "Where are the named/verifiable references?" | 5 of 7 |
| "Proof, not claim — show me the evidence" | 5 of 7 (mock-ups, certificates, tolerance specs, factory visits) |

| "Certificate names and ISO numbers, not just 'fire-rated'" | 3 of 7 |

### Surprises

- The procurement manager scores equal to the hospitality principal (3/3) — the brief's structural clarity and named entities (Hi Mobilya, SILADU) make it unusually RFP-ready. This is underexploited: a capability statement or scope matrix would turn this audience directly.
- "Fire compliance built-in" is the brief's most undercommunicated differentiator. The commercial developer and procurement manager respond to it strongly; the brief buries it in the differentiation table. Moving it to an earlier position — before the scope list — would raise the brief's utility for two key audiences.
- The UHNW homeowner persona cannot be reached through this brief. That is not a brief failure — it reflects the ICP's dual path: this persona comes through specifiers (architects, interior designers), not direct capability copy. The brief is the right instrument for the wrong audience here.

## Verdict

**Overall resonance: 2.1 / 3 (weighted average across panel)**

The Joinery & Manufacturing brief is well-calibrated for its two highest-weight audiences: hospitality principals and procurement teams. For them, the accountability model and the named manufacturing entities (Hi Mobilya, SILADU) deliver genuine differentiation clearly.

The brief falls short for the designer and craft audience because it frames joinery as a delivery mechanism, not a material discipline. One or two additions would close this gap: an explanation of what SILADU offers to a specifier, and one finish-level detail or material proof moment.

The residential segment is listed but not addressed. The homeowner persona is not reachable through B2B capability copy; that path runs through the interior designer. The interior designer brief, however, needs to work much harder on materiality.

**Three specific improvements, in priority order:**

1. **Promote "fire compliance built-in" to before the scope list.** It is the brief's most surprising, undercommunicated differentiator. It converts the commercial developer and procurement manager from "interested" to "add to longlist" if it appears in the opening section, not in a table row.

2. **Add one sentence explaining SILADU.** Something like: "SILADU is ORWOOD's in-house surface and materials division — veneers, laminates, solid surfaces, and textiles specified, sourced, and applied by the same team that designs and manufactures the joinery." This unlocks the brief for the architect and the interior designer without adding length.

3. **Add one proof layer for specifiers.** "We produce 1:1 mock-up panels and maintain full shop drawing review — the same team that specified the detail reviews the panel before production." This directly addresses the 5-of-7 "show me evidence" objection for the two specifier personas.
